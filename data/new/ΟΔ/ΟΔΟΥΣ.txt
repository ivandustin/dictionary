Etymology: The word "οδους" (odous) comes from the Greek language and means "tooth". It is derived from the Proto-Indo-European root "*dent-", which also gave rise to similar words in other Indo-European languages.

Grammar: In Greek, "οδους" (odous) is a noun of the masculine gender. It belongs to the second declension and has a genitive form "οδοντος" (odontos). It can be declined in various cases and numbers to indicate different grammatical functions.

Description: "Οδους" (odous) refers to a hard, calcified structure found in the jaws of many vertebrates, used for biting and chewing food. In humans, teeth are important for the process of mastication and play a crucial role in the digestive system. They are composed of enamel, dentin, and pulp, and are classified into different types, such as incisors, canines, premolars, and molars.
