Word: πληγας

Etymology:
The word πληγας (plēgas) is a Greek noun that means "wounds" or "strikes". It is derived from the verb πλήσσω (plēssō), which means "to strike" or "to hit". 

Grammar:
πληγας is a masculine noun in the nominative plural form. In Greek, nouns have different forms depending on their gender, number, and case. The nominative case is used for the subject of a sentence. 

Description:
πληγας is a word that refers to physical wounds or strikes. It can be used to describe injuries caused by weapons, accidents, or diseases. In ancient Greek literature, the word is often used metaphorically to describe emotional or psychological wounds. For example, in Homer's Iliad, the hero Achilles is described as having a "wound in his heart" (πληγὴν ἐν φρεσί, plēgēn en phresi) after the death of his friend Patroclus.
