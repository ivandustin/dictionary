Etymology: The word "μεσσιας" (mēssias) comes from the Hebrew word "מָשִׁיחַ" (māšîaḥ), meaning "anointed one" or "messiah". It was later adopted into Greek as "μεσσίας" (messias).

Grammar: In Greek, "μεσσίας" (messias) is a masculine noun in the nominative case. It is declined as follows:

Singular:
- Nominative: μεσσίας (messias)
- Genitive: μεσσίου (messiou)
- Dative: μεσσίᾳ (messia)
- Accusative: μεσσίαν (messian)

Plural:
- Nominative: μεσσίαι (messiai)
- Genitive: μεσσιῶν (messiōn)
- Dative: μεσσίαις (messiais)
- Accusative: μεσσίας (messias)

Description: The term "μεσσίας" (messias) refers to the messiah, a figure in Jewish and Christian beliefs who is expected to be anointed by God and bring salvation to humanity. In Christian theology, Jesus Christ is considered the messiah. The term can also be used more broadly to refer to any savior or deliverer.
