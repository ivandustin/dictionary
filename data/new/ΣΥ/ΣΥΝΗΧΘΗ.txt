Etymology: The word "συνήθης" (synēthēs) comes from the Greek roots "σύν" (syn), meaning "together," and "ἦθος" (ēthos), meaning "habit" or "custom." It can be translated as "habitual" or "customary."

Grammar: In Greek, "συνήθης" is an adjective that agrees in gender, number, and case with the noun it modifies. It has three forms: masculine, feminine, and neuter. For example, "συνήθης άνθρωπος" (synēthēs anthrōpos) means "habitual person" in the masculine form, while "συνήθης γυνή" (synēthēs gunē) means "habitual woman" in the feminine form.

Description: "Συνήθης" is used to describe something that is habitual, customary, or usual. It implies a regular or repeated occurrence. It can be applied to various contexts, such as behavior, practices, or traditions.
