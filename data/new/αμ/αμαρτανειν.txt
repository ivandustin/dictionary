Word: αμαρτανειν

Etymology:
The word αμαρτανειν (amartanein) is derived from the Greek word αμαρτία (amartia), which means "sin" or "error". The word is composed of the prefix α- (a-), which means "not" or "without", and μαρτυρία (marturia), which means "testimony" or "witness". Therefore, αμαρτανειν can be translated as "to be without testimony" or "to miss the mark".

αμαρτανειν is a verb in the present tense, active voice, and infinitive mood. It is the present infinitive form of the verb αμαρτάνω (amartano), which means "to sin" or "to make a mistake". The verb is conjugated in the present tense as follows:

- Εγώ αμαρτάνω (ego amartano) - I sin
- Εσύ αμαρτάνεις (esi amartaneis) - You sin
- Αυτός/Αυτή/Αυτό αμαρτάνει (autos/afti/afto amartanei) - He/She/It sins
- Εμείς αμαρτάνουμε (emeis amartanoume) - We sin
- Εσείς αμαρτάνετε (eseis amartanete) - You sin
- Αυτοί/Αυτές/Αυτά αμαρτάνουν (autoi/aftes/afta amartanoun) - They sin

Description:
αμαρτανειν is a word that is often used in religious contexts to refer to the act of sinning or making a mistake. It can also be used more broadly to describe any action that is considered to be wrong or incorrect. The word carries a sense of moral or ethical judgment, and is often used in discussions of right and wrong behavior.
