Word: υακινθος

Etymology:
The word υακινθος (hyacinthos) comes from the Greek mythological character Hyacinthus, who was a beautiful youth loved by the god Apollo. According to the myth, Hyacinthus was accidentally killed by Apollo, and from his blood grew a flower that was named after him - the hyacinth.

Grammar:
In Greek, υακινθος is a masculine noun of the second declension. Its nominative singular form is υακινθος, and its genitive singular form is υακινθου. The word can also be declined in the plural.

Description:
The hyacinth is a bulbous plant that produces fragrant flowers in a variety of colors, including blue, purple, pink, and white. It is native to the eastern Mediterranean region and is often grown as an ornamental plant. In Greek mythology, the hyacinth is associated with the god Apollo and is said to have grown from the blood of his beloved Hyacinthus. The flower has also been used in various cultures for medicinal and culinary purposes.
