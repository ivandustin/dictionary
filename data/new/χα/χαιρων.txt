Word: χαιρων

Etymology:
The word χαιρων (khairōn) is a present participle of the Greek verb χαίρω (khairō), which means "to rejoice" or "to be glad". It is derived from the Proto-Indo-European root *ǵʰer- ("to like, want").

Grammar:
In Greek, present participles are formed by adding the suffix -ων (-ōn) to the stem of the verb. They function as adjectives and describe an ongoing action or state. The word χαιρων is in the masculine nominative singular form.

Description:
The word χαιρων can be translated as "rejoicing" or "glad". It is often used in Greek literature and poetry to describe a person who is happy or joyful. For example, in Homer's Iliad, the god Apollo is described as "χαιρων ἐπὶ τοῖσιν ἔργοισιν" (khairōn epi toisin ergoisin), which means "rejoicing in his deeds". The word can also be used to express a greeting or farewell, similar to the English phrase "hello" or "goodbye".
