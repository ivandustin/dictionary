Word: αφθαρτον

Etymology:
The word αφθαρτον (aphtharton) is derived from the Greek word αφθαρτος (aphtharos), which means "imperishable" or "incorruptible". The prefix "α-" means "not" or "without", while "φθαρτος" (phthartos) means "perishable" or "corruptible". 

Grammar:
αφθαρτον is an adjective in the neuter gender, singular form. It is used to describe something that is not subject to decay or corruption. 

Description:
αφθαρτον is a word that describes something that is eternal, indestructible, and immune to decay or corruption. It is often used in a religious or philosophical context to describe the nature of the soul or the divine. In Greek mythology, the gods were often described as αφθαρτοι (aphthartoi), meaning they were immortal and could not be destroyed. The word can also be used to describe something that is pure, unspoiled, or untainted.
