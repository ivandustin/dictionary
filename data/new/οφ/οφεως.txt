Word: οφεως

Etymology:
The word οφεως (opheōs) is derived from the Greek word οφις (ophis), which means "snake" or "serpent". It is believed to have originated from the Proto-Indo-European root *h₁óphs, which also gave rise to the Latin word "serpens" and the English word "serpent".

Grammar:
Οφεως is a masculine noun in the Greek language. It is in the genitive case, which is used to indicate possession or relationship. In this case, it means "of the snake" or "belonging to the snake".

Description:
Οφεως is a word that refers to a snake or serpent. In Greek mythology, snakes were often associated with healing and wisdom, as well as with danger and deceit. The word is used in various contexts, such as in descriptions of the serpent in the Garden of Eden, or in the story of the Greek hero Perseus, who slayed the Gorgon Medusa, a woman with snakes for hair. The word can also be used metaphorically to describe someone who is sly or cunning.
