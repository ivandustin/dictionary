Word: παραβολαίς

Etymology: The word παραβολαίς comes from the Greek word παραβολή (parabolḗ), which means "comparison" or "analogy". It is derived from the prefix παρά (pará), meaning "beside" or "alongside", and the verb βάλλω (bállō), meaning "to throw".

Grammar: παραβολαίς is a feminine noun in the dative plural form in Greek. It is often translated as "parables" in English.

Description: παραβολαίς is a term used in the New Testament of the Bible to refer to the parables of Jesus. These are stories that Jesus used to teach moral and spiritual lessons to his followers. Some of the most well-known parables include the Good Samaritan, the Prodigal Son, and the Parable of the Sower. The use of parables was a common teaching method in ancient times, and Jesus used them to convey complex ideas in a way that was accessible to his audience.
