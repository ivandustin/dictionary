Word: πατριάρχας (patriarchas)

Etymology: The word πατριάρχας comes from the Greek words πατήρ (patir), meaning "father," and ἄρχων (archon), meaning "ruler" or "leader." It originally referred to the male head of a family or tribe, and later came to be used to describe the highest-ranking bishop in the Eastern Orthodox Church.

Grammar: In Greek, πατριάρχας is a masculine noun in the nominative case. Its plural form is πατριάρχαι (patriarchai). It is declined like other first-declension nouns, with the stem πατριαρχα-.

Description: πατριάρχας is a title used in various Eastern Christian churches to refer to the highest-ranking bishop or spiritual leader. In the Eastern Orthodox Church, the patriarch is the bishop of one of the five ancient patriarchates: Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem, and Moscow. The title is also used in the Oriental Orthodox Church and the Assyrian Church of the East. In secular usage, the term can also refer to a male head of a family or tribe.
