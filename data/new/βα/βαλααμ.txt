Word: βαλααμ

Etymology:
The word βαλααμ (Balaam) is a Greek transliteration of the Hebrew name בִּלְעָם (Bil'am). The name is composed of two Hebrew words: בִּלְעוֹם (bil'om), which means "not of the people," and אָם (am), which means "people." Therefore, the name Balaam means "not of the people" or "foreigner."

Grammar:
βαλααμ is a noun in the Greek language. It is a proper noun, which means it refers to a specific person, place, or thing. In this case, it refers to the person Balaam mentioned in the Bible.

Description:
Balaam was a prophet in the Old Testament of the Bible. He was hired by Balak, the king of Moab, to curse the Israelites. However, God spoke to Balaam and told him not to curse the Israelites. Balaam tried to curse them three times, but each time, he blessed them instead. Balaam is known for his talking donkey, which he rode on his way to meet Balak. The story of Balaam is found in the book of Numbers in the Bible.
