Word: βασιλευς

Etymology:
The word βασιλευς (basileus) is derived from the Greek word βασιλεύς, which means "king" or "ruler". It is believed to have originated from the Proto-Indo-European root word *h₃rḗǵs, which means "ruler" or "king".

Grammar:
In Greek, βασιλευς is a masculine noun of the second declension. It is declined as follows:

Singular:
Nominative: βασιλεύς (basileus) - "king"
Genitive: βασιλέως (basileos) - "of the king"
Dative: βασιλεῖ (basilei) - "to/for the king"
Accusative: βασιλέα (basilea) - "king" (direct object)
Vocative: βασιλεῦ (basileu) - "king" (addressed)

Plural:
Nominative: βασιλεῖς (basileis) - "kings"
Genitive: βασιλέων (basileon) - "of the kings"
Dative: βασιλεῦσι(ν) (basileusi(n)) - "to/for the kings"
Accusative: βασιλεῖς (basileis) - "kings" (direct object)
Vocative: βασιλεῖς (basileis) - "kings" (addressed)

Description:
βασιλευς is a Greek word that refers to a king or ruler. In ancient Greece, the βασιλευς was the highest authority in a city-state or kingdom. The term was also used to refer to the gods, who were considered the ultimate rulers of the universe. The word has been used in various contexts throughout history, including in the New Testament to refer to Jesus Christ as the "King of Kings". Today, the word is still used in modern Greek to refer to a king or queen.
