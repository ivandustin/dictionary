Word: καταπατησας

Etymology:
The word καταπατησας comes from the Greek verb καταπατέω (katapateo), which means "to trample down" or "to tread upon". It is derived from the prefix κατά (kata), meaning "down", and the verb πατέω (pateo), meaning "to tread" or "to walk".

Grammar:
καταπατησας is a verb in the aorist tense, which is used to describe a completed action in the past. It is also in the active voice, indicating that the subject of the sentence (the person doing the action) is performing the action. The word is in the second person singular form, meaning that it is addressing one person directly.

Description:
καταπατησας is a strong and forceful word that conveys the idea of trampling something underfoot or crushing it with great force. It can be used literally, to describe physically trampling something, or figuratively, to describe dominating or overpowering someone or something. In the New Testament, the word is often used in a moral or spiritual sense, to describe the act of trampling on God's laws or disregarding his authority.
