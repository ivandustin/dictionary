The word "καταχθονιων" (katachthoniōn) is a Greek adjective that refers to things or beings that belong to the underworld or the realm of the dead. 

Etymology: The word is derived from the Greek words "κατά" (kata), meaning "down" or "under," and "χθών" (chthōn), meaning "earth" or "land." 

Grammar: "καταχθονιων" is in the genitive plural form, which means it is used to describe multiple things or beings that belong to the underworld. 

Description: In Greek mythology, the underworld was ruled by Hades, the god of the dead, and was believed to be the final resting place for the souls of the deceased. The term "καταχθονιων" can be used to describe various creatures and objects associated with the underworld, such as the three-headed dog Cerberus, the ferryman Charon, and the river Styx.
