Word: κατειδωλον

Etymology:
The word κατειδωλον comes from the Greek words κατά (kata) meaning "down" or "against" and εἴδωλον (eidolon) meaning "image" or "idol". Therefore, κατειδωλον can be translated as "against idols" or "anti-idol".

Grammar:
κατειδωλον is a neuter noun in the singular form. It is declined as follows:

Nominative: κατειδωλον
Genitive: κατειδώλου
Dative: κατειδώλῳ
Accusative: κατειδωλον
Vocative: κατειδωλον

Description:
κατειδωλον is a word that appears in the New Testament of the Bible, specifically in 1 Corinthians 8:1-13. In this passage, the apostle Paul is discussing the issue of eating food that has been sacrificed to idols. He argues that while idols are not real gods, some people may still be influenced by them and therefore it is important to be considerate of others' beliefs. The word κατειδωλον is used to describe those who are against idols or idol worship.
