Word: φαρισαῖος (pharisaios)

Etymology: The word φαρισαῖος comes from the Hebrew word פְּרוּשִׁים (perushim), which means "separated ones" or "separatists". The Pharisees were a Jewish sect that emerged during the Second Temple period and were known for their strict adherence to Jewish law and traditions.

Grammar: φαρισαῖος is a masculine noun in the nominative case. It is declined as follows:

Singular:
- Nominative: φαρισαῖος
- Genitive: φαρισαίου
- Dative: φαρισαίῳ
- Accusative: φαρισαῖον
- Vocative: φαρισαῖε

Plural:
- Nominative: φαρισαῖοι
- Genitive: φαρισαίων
- Dative: φαρισαίοις
- Accusative: φαρισαίους
- Vocative: φαρισαῖοι

Description: φαρισαῖος refers to a member of the Pharisees, a Jewish sect that was active during the Second Temple period. The Pharisees were known for their strict adherence to Jewish law and traditions, and were often in conflict with other Jewish sects, such as the Sadducees and the Essenes. In the New Testament, the Pharisees are often portrayed as hypocritical and legalistic, and are frequently criticized by Jesus.
