Word: πειθεσθαι

Etymology:
The word πειθεσθαι (peithesthai) is a Greek verb that comes from the root word πείθω (peitho), which means "to persuade" or "to convince." The suffix -σθαι (-sthai) indicates that the verb is in the middle or passive voice, meaning that the subject is being acted upon or is acting on themselves.

Grammar:
πειθεσθαι is a present tense, middle or passive voice verb in Greek. It is often translated as "to be persuaded" or "to obey." The conjugation of this verb in the present tense is as follows:

- Singular:
  - 1st person: πείθομαι (peithomai) - I am persuaded
  - 2nd person: πείθεσαι (peithesai) - you are persuaded
  - 3rd person: πείθεται (peithetai) - he/she/it is persuaded

- Plural:
  - 1st person: πείθομεθα (peithometha) - we are persuaded
  - 2nd person: πείθεσθε (peithesthe) - you are persuaded
  - 3rd person: πείθονται (peithontai) - they are persuaded

Description:
πειθεσθαι is a verb that describes the act of being persuaded or convinced. It can also mean to obey or comply with someone or something. In Greek philosophy, the concept of πειθεσθαι was often associated with the idea of being rational and following reason, rather than being swayed by emotions or desires. The word is still used in modern Greek to describe the act of being convinced or persuaded.
