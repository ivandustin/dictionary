Word: πεισας

Etymology:
The word πεισας (peisas) is a form of the Greek verb πείθω (peitho), which means "to persuade" or "to convince". The verb is derived from the Proto-Indo-European root *bʰeydʰ- ("to persuade, convince"), which is also the source of the English words "bid" and "bide".

Grammar:
πεισας is the aorist active participle of the verb πείθω (peitho). As a participle, it functions as an adjective and describes a noun or pronoun. In this case, it describes a masculine singular noun or pronoun. The aorist tense indicates that the action of persuading or convincing has been completed in the past.

Description:
πεισας can be translated as "having persuaded" or "having convinced". It is often used in the New Testament to describe individuals who have been persuaded or convinced to believe in Jesus Christ. For example, in Acts 17:4, it is said that "some of them were persuaded (πεισαντες, peisantes) and joined Paul and Silas, as did a great many of the devout Greeks and not a few of the leading women".
