Word: πειθω

Etymology:
The word πειθω (peitho) is derived from the Greek verb πείθω (peithō), which means "to persuade" or "to convince". It is believed to have originated from the Proto-Indo-European root *bheidh-, which means "to urge, persuade".

Grammar:
πειθω is a Greek verb in the present tense, active voice, and indicative mood. It is conjugated as follows:

- Singular:
  - 1st person: πείθω (peitho) - I persuade
  - 2nd person: πείθεις (peitheis) - you persuade
  - 3rd person: πείθει (peithe) - he/she/it persuades

- Plural:
  - 1st person: πείθομεν (peithomen) - we persuade
  - 2nd person: πείθετε (peithete) - you persuade
  - 3rd person: πείθουσι (peithousi) - they persuade

Description:
πειθω is a common Greek verb that is used to express the act of persuading or convincing someone to do something. It is often used in a figurative sense, such as persuading someone to change their mind or convincing them to take a certain course of action. The verb can be used in a variety of contexts, including politics, business, and personal relationships.
