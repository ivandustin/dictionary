Word: πειθομαι

Etymology:
The word πειθομαι (peithomai) is derived from the Greek verb πείθω (peitho), which means "to persuade" or "to convince". The suffix -ομαι (-omai) is a reflexive suffix, which means that the verb is being done to oneself. Therefore, πειθομαι (peithomai) means "to be persuaded" or "to be convinced".

Grammar:
πειθομαι (peithomai) is a middle/passive verb in the present tense. It is conjugated as follows:

Singular:
- Πείθομαι (peithomai) - I am persuaded
- Πείθῃ (peithē) - You are persuaded
- Πείθεται (peithetai) - He/she/it is persuaded

Plural:
- Πείθομεθα (peithometha) - We are persuaded
- Πείθεσθε (peithesthe) - You (plural) are persuaded
- Πείθονται (peithontai) - They are persuaded

Description:
πειθομαι (peithomai) is a Greek verb that means "to be persuaded" or "to be convinced". It is often used in the context of persuasion or convincing someone to do something. The verb is commonly used in the New Testament of the Bible, where it is used to describe the process of being persuaded to believe in God or to follow Jesus. In modern Greek, the verb is still used in the same way, to describe the act of being persuaded or convinced of something.
