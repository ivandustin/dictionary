Etymology: The word "παρρησίαν" (parrhēsían) comes from the Greek word "πᾶς" (pas), meaning "all" or "every," and "ῥῆσις" (rhēsis), meaning "speech" or "utterance." It literally translates to "speaking everything" or "speaking freely."

Grammar: "Παρρησίαν" is a noun in the accusative case. In Greek, nouns change their form depending on their grammatical role in a sentence. The accusative case is used to indicate the direct object of a verb or the object of certain prepositions.

Description: "Παρρησίαν" refers to the concept of speaking openly, frankly, or boldly without fear or hesitation. It implies expressing one's thoughts, opinions, or beliefs freely, even if they are controversial or unpopular. In ancient Greece, "παρρησίαν" was highly valued as a virtue and a fundamental aspect of democracy. It was associated with political participation, freedom of speech, and the ability to challenge authority.
