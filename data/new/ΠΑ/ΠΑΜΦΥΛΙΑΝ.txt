Etymology: The word "παμφυλίαν" (pamphylian) comes from the Greek word "πᾶν" (pan), meaning "all," and "φύλον" (phylon), meaning "tribe" or "race." It refers to a region in ancient Anatolia (modern-day Turkey) called Pamphylia, which was inhabited by various tribes.

Grammar: "Παμφυλίαν" is a noun in the accusative case. In Greek, nouns change their form depending on their role in the sentence. The accusative case is used to indicate the direct object of a verb or the object of certain prepositions.

Description: "Παμφυλίαν" refers to the region of Pamphylia in ancient Anatolia. It was located on the southern coast of modern-day Turkey and bordered by Lycia to the west and Cilicia to the east. Pamphylia was known for its fertile land, diverse population, and important cities such as Perga and Side. The region played a significant role in the history and culture of ancient Greece and Rome.
