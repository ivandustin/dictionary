Etymology: The word "εκατονταρχος" (ekatontarchos) comes from the Greek words "εκατόν" (ekaton), meaning "hundred," and "αρχή" (arche), meaning "ruler" or "leader." It refers to a military rank in ancient Greece, equivalent to a centurion in the Roman army.

Grammar: In Greek, "εκατονταρχος" is a noun in the masculine gender. It is declined in the nominative case as "ο εκατονταρχος" (o ekatontarchos) and in the accusative case as "τον εκατονταρχο" (ton ekatontarcho).

Description: The term "εκατονταρχος" was used to describe a military officer who commanded a century, a unit of soldiers consisting of approximately 100 men. The rank of ekatontarchos was commonly found in the ancient Greek city-states and later in the Hellenistic and Roman armies. The ekatontarchos played a crucial role in leading and organizing troops in battle.
