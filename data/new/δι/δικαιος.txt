Word: δικαιος

Etymology:
The word δικαιος (dikaios) comes from the Greek root word δικη (dike), which means "justice" or "righteousness". The suffix -ος (-os) is a common ending in Greek that is used to form adjectives.

Grammar:
In Greek, δικαιος is an adjective that means "just" or "righteous". It can be used to describe a person who is morally upright or a decision that is fair and just. It is declined like most Greek adjectives, with different forms for masculine, feminine, and neuter genders, as well as different cases (nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, and vocative).

Description:
The word δικαιος is a key concept in Greek philosophy and religion, particularly in the works of Plato and Aristotle. It is often used to describe the ideal of justice and righteousness, and is associated with the concept of virtue. In the New Testament, the word is used to describe God's righteousness and the righteousness of believers. Overall, δικαιος is a powerful and important word in Greek that conveys a sense of moral goodness and uprightness.
