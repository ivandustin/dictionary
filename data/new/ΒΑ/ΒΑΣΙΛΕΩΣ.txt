Etymology: The word "βασιλεύς" (basileus) comes from the ancient Greek language. It is derived from the Proto-Indo-European root *h₂wes- meaning "to dwell" or "to reside". In ancient Greece, the term originally referred to the leader or ruler of a city-state or kingdom.

Grammar: In Greek grammar, "βασιλεύς" is a masculine noun of the second declension. It belongs to the nominative case, which is used for the subject of a sentence. The word can also be declined in other cases, such as genitive, dative, accusative, and vocative, depending on its role in a sentence.

Description: "Βασιλεύς" is the Greek word for "king" or "ruler". It refers to a person who holds supreme authority and power over a kingdom or territory. In ancient Greece, the role of a basileus varied depending on the city-state or region. They were often seen as the highest political and military authority, responsible for making important decisions and leading their people.
