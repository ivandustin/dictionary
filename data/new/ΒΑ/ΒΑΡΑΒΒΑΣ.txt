Etymology: The word "βαραββας" (varabbas) comes from the Aramaic word "בַּר־אַבָּא" (bar-abbā), which means "son of the father." It was later adopted into Greek as "βαραββας" (barabbas).

Grammar: In Greek, "βαραββας" is a masculine noun in the nominative case. It is declined as follows:
- Singular: βαραββας (barabbas)
- Plural: βαραββας (barabbas)

Description: In the New Testament of the Bible, "βαραββας" refers to a prisoner who was released by Pontius Pilate instead of Jesus Christ. This event is mentioned in the Gospels of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. The name "Barabbas" has become synonymous with someone who is pardoned or released from punishment.
