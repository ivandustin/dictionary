Etymology: The word "βασιλεύς" (basileus) comes from the ancient Greek language. It is derived from the Proto-Indo-European root *h₂wes- meaning "to dwell" or "to reside". In ancient Greece, the term originally referred to the leader or ruler of a city-state or kingdom.

Grammar: In Greek grammar, "βασιλεύς" is a masculine noun of the second declension. It belongs to the nominative case and can function as the subject of a sentence. It can also be declined to indicate different cases, such as genitive, dative, accusative, etc., depending on its role in a sentence.

Description: "Βασιλεύς" means "king" in Greek. It refers to a male monarch or ruler who holds supreme authority over a kingdom or territory. In ancient Greece, the role of a king was significant, as they were often seen as the highest authority and had political, military, and religious responsibilities. The term can also be used metaphorically to refer to someone who is highly respected or regarded as a leader in a particular field.
