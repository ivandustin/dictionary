Etymology: The word "פורים" (Purim) comes from the Hebrew word "פור" (pur), which means "lot" or "fate". It refers to the casting of lots by Haman, the antagonist in the biblical story of Esther, to determine the date for the extermination of the Jewish people.

Grammar: "פורים" (Purim) is a plural noun in Hebrew. It is derived from the singular form "פור" (pur) and is used to refer to the Jewish holiday of Purim.

Description: Purim is a festive Jewish holiday that commemorates the salvation of the Jewish people from Haman's plot to annihilate them in ancient Persia. It is celebrated on the 14th day of the Hebrew month of Adar (or Adar II in leap years). The holiday is characterized by reading the Book of Esther (Megillat Esther), dressing up in costumes, giving gifts to friends and the needy, and enjoying festive meals. It is a joyous holiday that emphasizes unity, gratitude, and the triumph of good over evil.
