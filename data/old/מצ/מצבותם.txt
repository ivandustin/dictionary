Etymology: The word "מִצְבּוֹת" (mitzvot) is a Hebrew word derived from the root צ.ו.ב (ts.v.b), which means "to command" or "to decree." It is the plural form of the word "מִצְוָה" (mitzvah), which means "commandment" or "good deed."

Grammar: "מִצְבּוֹת" is a noun in the Hebrew language. It is in the plural form and is used to refer to multiple commandments or good deeds.

Description: "מִצְבּוֹת" is a term used in Judaism to refer to the commandments or good deeds that are prescribed in the Torah and other Jewish texts. These commandments cover a wide range of religious, ethical, and ritual practices that guide the lives of Jewish individuals and communities. Observing the mitzvot is considered a fundamental aspect of Jewish religious practice and is believed to bring individuals closer to God and promote a just and moral society.
