Etymology: The word "מִצְבּוֹת" (mitzvot) is a Hebrew word derived from the root צ.ו.ב (ts.v.b), which means "to command" or "to decree." It is the plural form of the word "מִצְוָה" (mitzvah), which means "commandment" or "good deed."

Grammar: "מִצְבּוֹת" is a noun in the Hebrew language. It is in the plural form and is used to refer to multiple commandments or good deeds.

Description: "מִצְבּוֹת" is a term used in Judaism to refer to the commandments or religious obligations that are found in the Torah and other Jewish texts. These commandments cover a wide range of areas, including ethical behavior, ritual practices, and laws governing interpersonal relationships. Observing the mitzvot is considered a fundamental aspect of Jewish life and is seen as a way to connect with God and live a righteous life.
