Etymology: The word "מְצוֹה" (pronounced "mitzvah") comes from the Hebrew root צוֹה (tzavah), which means "to command" or "to order." In Jewish tradition, a mitzvah refers to a commandment or a religious duty.

Grammar: In Hebrew, "מְצוֹה" is a noun that is used to describe a commandment or a good deed. It can also refer to a religious obligation or a moral responsibility.

Description: A mitzvah is a concept in Judaism that encompasses both religious commandments and acts of kindness. It is considered a way to fulfill one's religious duties and to bring about positive actions in the world. Mitzvot can include a wide range of actions, such as observing the Sabbath, giving to charity, studying religious texts, and treating others with kindness and respect. The performance of mitzvot is seen as a way to connect with God and to contribute to the betterment of society.
