Etymology: The word "תְּשׁוּמָה" (teshuvah) comes from the Hebrew root שׁ.וּ.ב (sh.u.v), which means "to return" or "to repent." It is derived from the noun שׁוּב (shuv), which means "return."

Grammar: "תְּשׁוּמָה" is a feminine noun in the singular form. It is used in the Hebrew language to refer to the act of repentance or returning to a righteous path.

Description: "תְּשׁוּמָה" is a concept deeply rooted in Jewish tradition and religious practice. It is often associated with the process of introspection, self-reflection, and atonement. In Judaism, teshuvah is considered a vital part of spiritual growth and is practiced during the High Holy Days, especially on Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement. It involves acknowledging one's wrongdoings, expressing remorse, making amends, and committing to change one's behavior. Teshuvah is seen as a means of seeking forgiveness from both God and fellow human beings, as well as a way to restore harmony and balance in one's life.
