Etymology: The word "תְּשׁוּב" (teshuv) comes from the Hebrew root שׁוּב (shuv), which means "to return" or "to come back." It is derived from the verb שָׁב (shav), which means "to turn" or "to go back."

Grammar: "תְּשׁוּב" (teshuv) is a noun in the Hebrew language. It is in the singular form and is used to refer to the act of returning or coming back.

Description: "תְּשׁוּב" (teshuv) is a concept that holds great significance in Jewish tradition and religious practice. It is often translated as "repentance" or "returning to God." In Judaism, teshuvah is considered a process of self-reflection, remorse for past actions, and a commitment to change and improve oneself. It is a central theme during the High Holy Days, particularly on Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement. Teshuvah is seen as a way to seek forgiveness, reconcile with others, and restore one's relationship with God.
