Etymology: The word "תורה" (Torah) comes from the Hebrew root ירה (y-r-h), which means "to teach" or "to instruct." It is derived from the verb תּוֹרֶה (to-reh), which means "to guide" or "to direct."

Grammar: In Hebrew, "תורה" (Torah) is a feminine noun. It is usually used with definite articles such as "הַתּוֹרָה" (ha-to-rah) meaning "the Torah." It can also be used in the plural form as "תוֹרוֹת" (to-rot) meaning "Torahs" or "laws."

Description: The Torah is the central religious text of Judaism. It is composed of the Five Books of Moses: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy. The Torah contains the foundational laws, commandments, and teachings of Judaism. It is considered to be the word of God as revealed to Moses on Mount Sinai. The Torah is studied and revered by Jews as a guide for ethical and moral living, as well as a source of spiritual and historical knowledge.
