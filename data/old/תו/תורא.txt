Etymology: The word "תורא" (torah) comes from the Hebrew root ירה (y-r-h), which means "to teach" or "to instruct." It is derived from the verb ירה (yara), which means "to throw" or "to shoot." The Torah is considered the instruction or teaching given by God to the Jewish people.

Grammar: "תורא" (torah) is a noun in Hebrew and is typically used with definite articles such as "התורה" (ha-torah) meaning "the Torah." It can also be used in possessive forms such as "תורתי" (torati) meaning "my Torah" or "תורתך" (toratecha) meaning "your Torah."

Description: The Torah is the central religious text of Judaism. It is composed of the Five Books of Moses: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy. The Torah contains the foundational laws, commandments, and narratives of the Jewish faith. It is considered the most sacred and authoritative scripture in Judaism and is studied and revered by Jewish communities worldwide.
