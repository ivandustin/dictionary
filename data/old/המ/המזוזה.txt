Etymology: The word "המזוזה" (HaMezuzah) comes from the Hebrew root "זוז" (zuz), which means "to move" or "to shake". It is derived from the verb "מזז" (mazaz), which means "to move" or "to shake". The word "המזוזה" (HaMezuzah) is a noun form of this root and refers to a small parchment scroll containing biblical verses that is placed in a case and affixed to the doorpost of a Jewish home.

Grammar: In Hebrew, "המזוזה" (HaMezuzah) is a feminine noun. It is preceded by the definite article "ה" (Ha), which means "the". The word "מזוזה" (Mezuzah) is singular, but it can also be used in the plural form "מזוזות" (Mezuzot).

Description: The Mezuzah is a significant symbol in Jewish tradition and is considered a mitzvah (commandment) to affix it to the doorposts of Jewish homes. It contains handwritten verses from the Torah, specifically Deuteronomy 6:4-9 and 11:13-21. The Mezuzah serves as a reminder of Jewish identity, faith, and the importance of living a moral and ethical life. It is often placed on the right side of the doorpost, tilted inward, and is kissed or touched when entering or leaving the home as a sign of respect and acknowledgment of God's presence.
