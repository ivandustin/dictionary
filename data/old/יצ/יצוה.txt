Etymology: The word "יצוה" (pronounced "mitzvah") comes from the Hebrew root צ.ו.ה (ts.w.h), which means "to command" or "to instruct." In Jewish tradition, a mitzvah refers to a commandment or a religious duty.

Grammar: "יצוה" is a noun in the Hebrew language. It is written in the singular form and belongs to the feminine gender. In plural form, it becomes "מצוות" (mitzvot).

Description: In Judaism, a mitzvah is a religious obligation or a good deed that is commanded by God. It encompasses a wide range of actions, including both ritualistic and ethical practices. Mitzvot are considered to be a way for individuals to connect with God and fulfill their spiritual duties. They cover various aspects of life, such as prayer, study, acts of kindness, and observance of religious rituals and holidays. Mitzvot are an integral part of Jewish life and are seen as a means of bringing holiness into the world.
