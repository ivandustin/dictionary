Etymology: The word "עִבְרִית" (Hebrew) comes from the Hebrew root ע-ב-ר (Ayin-Bet-Resh), which means "to cross over" or "to pass through." It is believed to refer to the Hebrew language as a means of communication that has been passed down through generations.

Grammar: Hebrew is a Semitic language and has a complex grammatical structure. It is written from right to left and consists of consonants and vowel points. Hebrew nouns have gender (masculine or feminine) and number (singular or plural), and they are declined accordingly. Verbs are conjugated based on tense, person, and number. Hebrew also has a system of prefixes and suffixes that indicate various grammatical functions.

Description: Hebrew is an ancient language that has been spoken for thousands of years. It is the language of the Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) and is considered the sacred language of Judaism. Modern Hebrew, also known as Israeli Hebrew, is the official language of the State of Israel and is spoken by millions of people worldwide. It is a vibrant and evolving language that has been revived and adapted for modern usage. Hebrew is known for its rich literary tradition, including poetry, prose, and religious texts.
